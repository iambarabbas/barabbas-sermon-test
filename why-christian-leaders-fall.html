Why Christian Leaders Keep Falling (And Why Accountability Won't Fix It)

By Pastor Matt Smith · March 10, 2026 · 9 min read

I've been a Christian since 2002. In that time, I've watched more prominent pastors fall than I can count.

I'd be at a conference, thinking, "That guy's my favorite preacher," only to find out later he'd been having affairs for eight years. Early in my ministry, people would say I reminded them of Mark Driscoll. Then he imploded. I used to love Philip Yancey's book The Jesus I Never Knew—then discovered he'd been hiding an eight-year affair into his 80s.

We've seen pastors embezzle money, flee to foreign countries with prostitutes, and spend church funds on things I won't mention here. One of the first guys I ever shared a stage with—a church planting leader—was caught spending raised funds on prostitutes. His defense? "I was doing research."

Every time this happens, we hear the same tired response: "The church really needs better accountability structures."

And every time, I want to ask: Do we really think that's the problem?

The Accountability Myth

When a leader falls, our instinct is always the same: blame the systems. We say things like:

  • "They didn't have enough accountability."
  • "Who was holding him accountable?"
  • "We shouldn't give people such big platforms."
  • "Maybe he didn't have enough quiet time."
  • "The workload was too heavy."

And then we reach for solutions: more oversight, more structures, more checks and balances, more committees. Someone writes another book on leadership. Another seminar on pastoral health. Another accountability app.

Here's the uncomfortable truth: The problem with these fallen leaders wasn't their organization or their structures. The problem was that they weren't men of God.

Imagine having all those accountability structures—and still having those same men leading. Would you want that? Would you want a pastor who would sin if given the chance, but is only restrained by external controls? Is that what we're aiming for?

Israel Had Everything—And Still Failed

This isn't a new problem. In Deuteronomy 1, Moses is preaching to the second generation of Israelites—the children of those who walked through the Red Sea, saw the plagues, ate manna from heaven, and watched Pharaoh's army drown.

Moses reminds them of what their parents had:

  • Clear revelation — God's promises to Abraham, Isaac, and Jacob, repeated and confirmed over 400 years
  • Miraculous provision — the Exodus, the parted sea, daily manna, water from rock
  • Wise organization — commanders of thousands, hundreds, fifties, and tens
  • Godly leadership — Moses himself, plus appointed judges and elders
  • Direct commands — "Go in and take possession of the land"

They had everything. Every resource. Every structure. Every promise confirmed.

And when it came time to enter the Promised Land? They sent spies—the very leaders Moses had appointed, the wise and experienced men—and those leaders came back terrified. They saw giants and forgot God's faithfulness.

"Yet you would not go up, but rebelled against the command of the Lord your God." — Deuteronomy 1:26

Israel had everything necessary—except faith.

We Don't Need Better Methods. We Need Better Men.

Moses' sermon in Deuteronomy isn't a leadership seminar. He's not teaching organizational principles. He's making a devastating point: all that structure, all that clarity, all that accountability—and they still fell.

The spies who brought back the bad report weren't random troublemakers. They were "heads of the people of Israel" (Numbers 13:3). They were the wise guys. The execs. The council. The accountability structure itself.

And they failed because they lacked faith.

Only Caleb—one man among the twelve—had what Numbers 14:24 calls "a different spirit." He "followed God fully." That's why he alone (along with Joshua) entered the land.

The distinction wasn't methodology. It was the man.

God didn't say, "Caleb had better systems." He said Caleb had a different spirit. He followed God fully when everyone else calculated the risks and chose fear.

Why We Keep Reaching for Systems

So why do we keep defaulting to structural solutions? Why, every time a pastor falls, do we immediately talk about accountability instead of character?

Here's the uncomfortable answer: we do the same thing in every area of life.

When our marriage struggles, we look for techniques. Date night tips. Communication frameworks. Love languages. When parenting gets hard, we buy another book. When we fail spiritually, we download another app, join another program, try another method.

We do this because if the problem is systemic, it's fixable with effort. If the problem is us—our hearts, our faith, our character—that's much harder to face.

"The reason your marriage is hard is because you're in it."

That sounds harsh. But it's true. You're a sinner who married another sinner. The kids you're raising are sinners being raised by sinners. Your church is led by sinners shepherding sinners.

Systems can't fix that. Only Christ can.

What the Church Actually Needs

I'm not saying organization doesn't matter. Moses appreciated Jethro's wisdom about delegating. Our church has elders, small groups, and structure for good reasons.

But here's what we have to understand: if the Holy Spirit didn't exist and the church could still function, what in the world are we doing?

The church is not a business. It's not a nonprofit with religious branding. It's the body of Christ—a living organism, the temple of God, the bride of Christ. None of those images sound like corporate org charts.

In Ephesians 4, Paul tells us that God gave the church "apostles, prophets, evangelists, shepherds and teachers" to equip the saints. Notice what he didn't give: he didn't give us organizational structures. He didn't give us a pope with succession planning. He didn't give us HR departments.

He gave us people. Gifts in the form of godly men and women.

And what do those people need? Not more management training. They need to be people of faith—like Caleb, who had "a different spirit."

The Real Solution: Put On Christ

Here's where the gospel transforms this whole conversation.

The good news isn't just that God gave us a better man to imitate. It's that He gave us a perfect man to inhabit.

Jesus lived the life we couldn't live. He died the death we couldn't face. He rose again, ascended to heaven, and sent His Spirit to dwell in everyone who puts their faith in Him.

The wilderness generation had Moses, clear promises, and great organization. We have all that—plus Jesus living in us, working through us.

"Finally, be strong in the Lord and in the strength of his might. Put on the whole armor of God, that you may be able to stand against the schemes of the devil." — Ephesians 6:10-11

This isn't a self-help pep talk. It's not "try harder." It's the recognition that we're creatures who need to depend on our Creator. We can't white-knuckle our way to holiness. We need to put on Christ—daily, moment by moment.

You Already Know What To Do

Let me get personal for a moment.

For many of you, the problem isn't that the Bible is confusing. The stuff you struggle with isn't the hard parts of Scripture—it's the direct ones:

  • Rejoice always.
  • Give thanks in all circumstances.
  • Forgive as you've been forgiven.
  • Bless those who curse you.
  • Love your spouse sacrificially.
  • Share the gospel with that coworker.

You don't need another book. You don't need another class. You don't need one more counseling session before you can take the next step.

You already know what the next step is.

You know you need to forgive that person. You know you need to apologize. You know you need to tell the truth. You know you need to end that sin you keep protecting. You know you need to lead your family. You know you need to give sacrificially.

What you're actually doing is shopping for counsel—hoping someone will tell you something easier. Another perspective. Another conversation. Another podcast.

Courageous faith is trusting Christ enough to obey Him when obedience feels like losing.

It may cost you your pride, your argument, your comfort, your reputation, your control. That's where your faith is proven.

Turn and Take Your Journey

Moses told Israel: "You have stayed long enough at this mountain. Turn and take your journey."

Many of us have stayed long enough on the mountain of these truths. We've heard them over and over. We've collected the knowledge. We've organized our systems.

It's time to courageously believe them.

It's time to turn from the mountain and take the journey God has prepared for you—not with better techniques, but with the Christ who lives in you and wants to work through you.

The church doesn't need better accountability structures. It needs men and women who actually believe that Jesus is who He says He is—and live like it.

Put on Christ. Today. Right now. Not after one more conversation. Not after one more book.

Now.

The Bottom Line: When Christian leaders fall, the problem isn't usually a lack of systems—it's a lack of faith. We don't need better methods; we need men and women who actually believe God and follow Him fully, like Caleb did. And that's only possible when we stop trusting in techniques and start putting on Christ.
