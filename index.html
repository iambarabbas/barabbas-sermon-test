What is Oneness Pentecostalism? A Biblical Response to Modalism

By Pastor Matt Smith · March 10, 2026 · 8 min read

If you've spent any time in charismatic Christian circles, you've likely encountered Oneness Pentecostalism—even if you didn't recognize it. This movement, sometimes called "Jesus Only" theology, is one of the fastest-growing religious movements in America. And while its adherents use familiar Christian language, they deny one of the most foundational doctrines of the faith: the Trinity.

In this article, we'll examine what Oneness Pentecostalism teaches, how it differs from biblical Christianity, and how to respond when you encounter these beliefs.

The Origins of Oneness Pentecostalism

Oneness Pentecostalism emerged from the Assemblies of God in the early 20th century. Between 1913 and 1916, several Pentecostal leaders began teaching that baptism must be performed "in the name of the Lord Jesus Christ" only—not in the name of the Father, Son, and Holy Spirit as Jesus commanded in Matthew 28:19.

This wasn't merely a baptismal formula dispute. These leaders took the position further: they argued that the Father, Son, and Holy Spirit are not three distinct persons, but rather three different "modes" or roles that the one God (Jesus) assumes at different times.

Key Fact: The Assemblies of God formally rejected Oneness theology at their 1916 General Council. This led to 156 ministers leaving to form what would become the Oneness Pentecostal movement.

Today, the largest Oneness organization is the United Pentecostal Church International (UPC), which encompasses approximately 75% of all Oneness Pentecostals. The movement includes over 5 million adherents worldwide and was growing at 10-15% annually. High-profile preachers like T.D. Jakes have been associated with Oneness theology, though Jakes has attempted to distance himself from the label while maintaining language consistent with modalism.

What Does Oneness Pentecostalism Teach?

Oneness Pentecostalism has three primary doctrinal distinctives that set it apart from orthodox Christianity:

1. Jesus is the Father, Son, and Holy Spirit

Oneness theology teaches that there is only one person in the Godhead—Jesus—who wears different "hats" or assumes different roles. When God acts as Creator, he's called "Father." When he takes on human flesh, he's called "Son." When he's active in believers' hearts, he's called "Holy Spirit."

This view, technically called modalism, has been rejected by the Christian church since the early centuries. It was formally condemned as heresy because it fundamentally distorts who God is and undermines the entire narrative of Scripture.

"If Jesus and the Father are the same person, then the Cross becomes cosmic theater. God isn't actually sacrificing His Son—He's just playacting at sacrifice. The profound love displayed when 'God so loved the world that he gave his only Son' becomes meaningless."

2. Denial of the Trinity

Oneness Pentecostals characterize the Trinity as "belief in three gods" and claim it's illogical. They point out (correctly) that the word "Trinity" doesn't appear in the Bible. However, neither does "Oneness Pentecostalism"—and that argument proves nothing about whether the concept is biblical.

The Trinity doctrine states:

  • There is one God (Deuteronomy 6:4)
  • The Father is God
  • The Son (Jesus) is God
  • The Holy Spirit is God
  • The Father is not the Son; the Son is not the Spirit; the Spirit is not the Father

This is not three Gods (tritheism). It's one God existing eternally in three distinct persons. Is it difficult to fully comprehend? Absolutely. But being beyond full human comprehension doesn't make something illogical—it makes it transcendent, which is exactly what we'd expect from an infinite God.

3. Salvation Requires Works

Perhaps most concerning for those who hold to the Gospel of grace, Oneness Pentecostalism teaches that salvation requires:

  • Faith in Jesus
  • Repentance
  • Water baptism specifically in the name of "Jesus only"
  • Speaking in tongues as evidence of receiving the Holy Spirit
  • Maintaining a "life of holiness" (no smoking, drinking, dancing, movies, television, jewelry, makeup for women, cutting women's hair, etc.)

If you've been baptized in the name of "the Father, Son, and Holy Spirit"—as Christians have been for 2,000 years—Oneness Pentecostals would say you're not truly saved and need to be rebaptized.

A Biblical Response

The Father and Son Are Distinct Persons

Scripture is relentlessly clear that the Father and Son are distinct persons in relationship with each other:

  • The Father sent the Son (John 3:16, Galatians 4:4)
  • The Father and Son love each other (John 3:35, 5:20)
  • They speak to each other (John 11:41-42, 17:1-26)
  • Jesus is our advocate with the Father (1 John 2:1)

None of this makes sense if Jesus is the Father. You don't send yourself. You don't advocate with yourself. When Jesus prayed in the garden, "Not my will, but yours be done," was he talking to himself? When Jesus cried out, "My God, my God, why have you forsaken me?"—was he forsaking himself?

The Oneness interpretation makes the Bible's most profound moments absurd.

John 1:1 Establishes Eternal Distinction

"In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God, and the Word was God." — John 1:1

The Greek preposition translated "with" (πρός, pros) indicates intimate, face-to-face fellowship. Before creation, the Word (Jesus) existed in eternal relationship with the Father—not as the Father wearing a different hat.

Matthew 3:16-17 Shows All Three Persons Simultaneously

At Jesus' baptism, we see all three persons of the Trinity at once: Jesus is in the water, the Spirit descends as a dove, and the Father speaks from heaven. The Oneness response? Jesus was doing all of this to himself—including throwing his voice from heaven.

This strains credulity far more than the straightforward Trinitarian reading.

Baptism in "Jesus' Name" vs. The Trinitarian Formula

Oneness Pentecostals build much of their case on Acts 2:38, where Peter tells the crowd to "be baptized in the name of Jesus Christ." But context matters: Peter was preaching to Jews who already believed in the Father and the Spirit—they specifically denied that Jesus was the Messiah. Peter was calling them to acknowledge the one they rejected.

This doesn't contradict Matthew 28:19, where Jesus explicitly commands baptism "in the name of the Father and of the Son and of the Holy Spirit." Notice: one name, three persons—reflecting the unity and distinction of the Trinity.

Why This Matters

Some might wonder why we should care about these theological distinctions. Isn't this just academic debate?

Not at all. Getting God wrong has consequences:

  • It distorts the Gospel. If salvation requires specific baptismal formulas and speaking in tongues, we're no longer saved by grace through faith—we're saved by works. This is the error Paul condemned in Galatians.
  • It undermines God's love. The Trinity reveals a God who has existed in eternal, self-giving love between Father, Son, and Spirit. A modalistic God has no one to love until he creates.
  • It robs the Cross of its meaning. The sacrifice of the Son by the Father—planned before the foundation of the world—demonstrates the depths of God's love. Modalism reduces this to divine playacting.

As believers, we're called to worship God as he has revealed himself—not as we might find more convenient or easier to understand. The Trinity is difficult, but it's what Scripture teaches. We can apprehend it without fully comprehending it. And that's okay.

The Bottom Line: Oneness Pentecostalism uses Christian vocabulary but teaches a different God and a different Gospel. If you encounter these beliefs, respond with grace and truth—pointing people to the Scriptures and the God who saves by grace alone, through faith alone, in Christ alone.
